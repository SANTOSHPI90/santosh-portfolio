Social Research

1. Theoretical Foundations (The Sociological Lens)
Before touching data, a researcher must understand the structures they are measuring.
Social Stratification: An interest in how class, race, gender, and education levels create inequalities in society.
Institutional Analysis: Studying how systems like healthcare, government, and religion influence individual choices.
Micro vs. Macro Perspectives: The ability to switch between looking at individual interactions (Symbolic Interactionism) and large-scale societal shifts (Functionalism or Conflict Theory).

2. Quantitative Data Analysis
This is the "hard science" side of social research, turning human behavior into measurable patterns.
Statistical Literacy: Interest in moving beyond averages to understand correlation vs. causation, significance levels, and regression analysis.
Survey Methodology: Designing unbiased questions and understanding sampling techniques (e.g., random vs. stratified sampling) to ensure a study represents the real world.
Demographics: Using large datasets (like Census data) to track population trends, migration, and aging.

3. Qualitative Depth
Data isn't always numbers; sometimes it’s language, observation, and context.
Ethnography & Observation: An interest in "immersion"—spending time within a community to understand their lived reality from the inside.
Thematic Coding: The skill of taking hours of interviews or pages of text and identifying recurring patterns or "themes."
Content Analysis: Analyzing media, historical documents, or social media trends to see how cultural narratives change over time.

4. Technical & Analytical Tools
Modern social research requires a "tech stack" similar to data science but applied to human problems.
Statistical Software: Learning tools like SPSS, Stata, or R to process complex social surveys.
Python for Social Science: Using libraries like Pandas or Matplotlib to scrape social media data or visualize demographic shifts.
Data Visualization: The ability to tell a story with data, making complex social trends understandable to the public or policymakers.

5. Ethics and Reflexivity
Research Ethics: A deep commitment to participant anonymity, informed consent, and "doing no harm."
Reflexivity: The self-awareness to recognize your own biases as a researcher and how they might influence your interpretation of the data.
